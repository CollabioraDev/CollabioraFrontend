*What does it actually mean to have anxiety, depression, or ADHD — and when does "feeling off" become something worth taking seriously?*

In Episode 3 of Hot Mic, we sat down with a psychiatrist for one of the most grounded, stigma-free conversations we've had yet. No fearmongering. No oversimplification. Just an honest look at how our minds work, when they struggle, and what actually helps.

---

## Anxiety & Depression: When Does It Become a Disorder?

Almost everyone feels anxious or low at some point. So how do you know when it crosses a line? The answer isn't about the intensity of the feeling — it's about whether it's getting in the way of your life.

The clinical threshold is function. Too anxious to leave the house. Too low to get out of bed. When symptoms consistently interfere with your day-to-day — and have been doing so for roughly four weeks or more — that warrants a proper evaluation.

Anxiety and depression often travel together. They can coexist or trigger one another, which is why clinicians frequently write them together. They fall under different diagnostic categories — mood disorders versus fear-related disorders — but in practice, they're rarely neat and separate.

One shift worth noting: social media has widened awareness of mental health in a real way. More people now recognise that what they feel isn't what they're *supposed* to feel. That recognition is part of why prevalence appears to be rising — not just because things are getting worse, but because people are finally naming what was always there.

## Medication vs Therapy: It's Not Either/Or

Here's a framing shift that came up early in the conversation and stuck: the question isn't *therapy or medication* — it's *what does this person need right now?*

The old model — try therapy first, medication only as a last resort — is outdated. Both are valid tools. Some people benefit most from working through thought patterns and behaviours with a therapist. Others are so deep in the experience that they need medication just to get their head above water first.

> PULL QUOTE: "Medication can get you out of the phase of drowning — but it doesn't get you fully out of the water. You still have to work on the other parts that led you there."
> — Hot Mic, Episode 3

Psychiatry uses what's called a biopsychosocial model. Mental illness has biological roots (genetics, brain chemistry), psychological roots (thought patterns, trauma), and social roots (isolation, chronic stress). Medication addresses the biology. Therapy, lifestyle, and connection address the rest. You usually need more than one.

## What You Need to Know About Antidepressants

If you're starting an SSRI — the most commonly prescribed antidepressant — there are a few things your doctor should tell you, and sometimes doesn't.

**They take time.** Full effect typically takes four to six weeks. Dosage often needs adjusting. This requires patience, and it's normal to feel like nothing is happening at first.

**Coming off isn't simple.** The general guidance is to stay on medication for about six months after symptoms resolve before starting to taper — and even then, withdrawal effects are real and need careful management. Never stop abruptly without talking to your doctor.

**This is the most important one:** In young people especially, SSRIs can sometimes increase suicidal thoughts before they start to help. Patients must be warned about this before starting. If your doctor doesn't bring it up — ask. If you experience it, seek help immediately.

> CALLOUT: There is no stigma in staying on medication long-term. Some conditions require it. These medications, used correctly, can be life-saving.

## ADHD: Two Waves, One Conversation

Something interesting is happening with ADHD diagnosis right now. Two groups are being identified simultaneously: younger people flagged through schools, and adults in their 30s and 40s who are realising — often with enormous relief — that the symptoms they've always had actually explain a lot about who they are. Both are valid. Both deserve careful assessment.

What often gets missed in this conversation is everything *before* medication. Sleep regulation. Eating regularly. Short bursts of focused activity with rest in between. These interventions aren't sexy, but they matter — and they're not talked about enough.

There's also a harder question worth sitting with: *How much of what I'm experiencing is possible ADHD, and how much of it is trying to meet a standard that was never achievable?* We live in an attention economy engineered around 15-second reels. That environment is training all of our brains — not just neurodivergent ones — to struggle with sustained focus.

> CALLOUT: What stimulant medication does to a neurotypical person — increased energy, hyperactivity — is essentially the opposite of what it does for someone with ADHD. Most patients describe it simply: "My mind is just quiet for the first time."

If you are on ADHD medication, monitor your health at minimum every six months — heart rate, blood pressure, height, weight, blood tests. Stimulants affect the cardiovascular system and appetite. And you don't need to take them every single day; breaks on weekends or holidays are completely fine.

## Neurodivergence: Difference, Not Deficiency

ADHD, autism, and learning disabilities can and do overlap. The term AuDHD — autism and ADHD together — has gained traction because for many people, a dual diagnosis finally explains an experience that neither label alone captured fully.

Formal assessment for any of this requires multiple sources: your own account, family history, early development, school records. It's not a checklist you complete in a GP appointment.

For autism specifically, there's limited medication — but lifestyle modifications and, perhaps more importantly, *acceptance* matter enormously. The way you interface with the world is slightly different. That doesn't need to be medicated away. It speaks to the extraordinary diversity of the human brain.

## Going Back to Basics

One of the most quietly powerful moments in this episode was a simple observation: as a society, we've drifted away from the things that have always regulated us as a species. Togetherness. Third spaces. Making music. Moving our bodies. Being physically present with other people.

These aren't nice extras. They're foundational. And returning to some of them holds more answers to the rising global mental health burden than we give them credit for.

## If You're Struggling

Don't feel shy about reaching out. These conditions can lead to some very dark places — and when they do, help is available.

Suicide & crisis support:
- **US:** 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline — call or text 988
- **UK:** Samaritans — call 116 123

*Be yourself. Be happy with who you are. But if you're struggling — reach out.*
