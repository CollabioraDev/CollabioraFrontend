*What does it take to live with a chronic skin condition that everyone has an opinion about — but nobody has a real answer for? In this episode, we get personal.*

This one is different. In Episode 4 of Hot Mic, we stepped away from the clinical and got personal. One of our own sat down to share a 30-year journey with severe atopic dermatitis — the medical name for eczema — told alongside his partner. It's a story about chronic pain, exhaustion, misinformation, medical gatekeeping, and eventually, finding something that actually works.

If you have eczema, this episode is for you. And honestly, even if you don't — it's a story about what it means to advocate for your own health when the system keeps handing you the bare minimum.

---

## Born With It

Atopic dermatitis isn't something you develop. For many people, it's just always been there. Our guest was born with dry patches on his arms and spent the next three decades navigating flares, triggers, and a revolving door of treatments that mostly didn't work.

The experience he describes is one that anyone with a chronic condition will recognise: your body becomes a puzzle you're expected to solve yourself, with almost no guidance. Doctors have generic tools to stop the itching — but nothing that addresses the underlying problem. And meanwhile, everyone around you has a suggestion.

> CALLOUT: "The most annoying part of eczema is that people can see it. So everybody is worried, everybody has advice — and you're just exhausted. You don't want to talk about eczema again."

That exhaustion is real, and it's under-discussed. Living with a visible chronic condition means never getting a break from other people's concern, however well-meaning.

---

## The Treatment Treadmill

Before finding something that worked, the list of things tried was long. Tar cream. Oat baths. Salt baths — which, as our guest puts it, felt like throwing acid on an open wound. Steroid creams that worked for a week and then required repeating, indefinitely.

The steroid creams deserve their own mention. They're effective in the short term. Doctors prescribe them freely. But they also cause the skin to thin over time — and they can be addictive. The condition known as topical steroid withdrawal is real: stop using the creams after long-term use, and your skin can inflame across your entire body in what some people describe as a years-long detox. Our guest and his partner went down that rabbit hole, tried the withdrawal process, and lived through a year of it before reaching a breaking point.

> CALLOUT: Topical steroid withdrawal can take up to three years. During that time, skin can become severely inflamed across the whole body. It is a recognised condition — but rarely discussed openly by the doctors prescribing the creams in the first place.

The turning point came on a holiday in Portugal. They woke up to a pillow soaked through from open wounds on his head. His partner ran downstairs asking for a doctor. That was the moment it became clear: something had to change.

---

## Fighting the System to Get the Right Treatment

After years of trying everything, they finally sought out dupilumab — a targeted biologic injection that works by blocking the specific immune signal that triggers eczema flares. It doesn't use steroids. It doesn't suppress the whole immune system. It targets one particular protein, precisely.

Getting it, however, wasn't straightforward.

At the dermatologist's office, they were pushed back toward steroid creams — again. The reason? Insurance. Dupilumab costs around $4,000 a month without coverage. Healthcare systems, whether through insurance in the US or the public system in the Netherlands, are incentivised to exhaust cheaper options first. Patients aren't always told this is what's happening. They just experience it as being sent backwards.

> PULL QUOTE: "We really had to advocate. We said: we've been there. We've tried everything. We want dupilumab."
> — Hot Mic, Episode 4

That advocacy mattered. They pushed. They got it.

---

## What Dupilumab Actually Feels Like

The first appointment involved two shots at once. He nearly fainted. The idea of then self-injecting at home — into the stomach or the leg, once or twice a month — felt like an enormous mental barrier.

It got easier. The pen-style injector means you don't see the needle. You press it in, wait ten seconds, and it's done. The early months came with side effects — dry eyes that required daily drops, sensitivity to alcohol. But those faded.

Four years on: no flares. No seasonal allergies. No reaction to dogs — they now have one.

> CALLOUT: "I'm not allergic to dogs anymore. I don't have seasonal allergies anymore. I'm a different person."

That's not a small thing. That's thirty years of daily discomfort, resolved.

---

## What This Episode Is Really About

Beneath the eczema story is a bigger one — about what happens when patients are under-informed, when stigma around medication keeps people from treatments that could help, and when the financial interests of insurers quietly shape the care people receive.

Our guest was resistant to dupilumab for a long time — not because he didn't want to get better, but because of how it was presented to him. A shot. Experimental. Something to be wary of. That framing, combined with a broader cultural skepticism toward long-term medication use, cost him years.

The information was always there. The treatment existed. The barrier wasn't medical — it was a combination of stigma, poor communication, and a system structured to delay.

---

## If You're In the Trenches Right Now

Our guest's advice, for anyone currently living with eczema or another chronic condition:

01 — Listen to your doctor — but know that insurance interests can shape what they offer you first. The cheapest option isn't always the most appropriate one for where you are.

02 — Be aware of topical steroid dependency. If you've been on them long-term, research topical steroid withdrawal so you understand the full picture before making decisions.

03 — Don't let fear of injections or long-term medication hold you back if you've already tried everything else. Stigma around chronic medication use is real — and it causes real harm.

04 — Advocate loudly. Name what you've already tried. Ask directly about newer treatments. Push back if you're being sent backwards through options you've already exhausted.

05 — There is no universal answer for eczema — what works for one person won't work for another. But that's a reason to keep looking, not a reason to stop.

> PULL QUOTE: "There is a way out. I was walking on a minefield every day — but there is a way out."
> — Hot Mic, Episode 4
