*In an age where a single TikTok can go viral faster than a peer-reviewed journal can clear ethics review — how do we know what to believe about our health?*

In this episode of Hot Mic, we got into the weeds on something that affects every single one of us: research literacy. Specifically, why most people don't have it, why the internet takes advantage of that, and what we can actually do about it.

The conversation started — as all great ones do — with bread and tomatoes. But it quickly moved somewhere more serious: the yawning gap between what science actually says and what ends up on your For You page.

---

## The Problem No One Talks About

Here's an uncomfortable truth: most of us, even those with higher education, don't know how to read a research paper. And social media has weaponized that gap. Studies get cited in Instagram captions and podcast clips stripped of all their caveats — no sample sizes, no study type, no replication context. Just the dramatic headline.

Our guests admitted something surprisingly refreshing: *they* fall for it too. Even a practicing physician. Even someone trained in research methodology. Critical thinking isn't a superpower some people are born with — it's a skill, and it takes real effort to apply consistently.

> CALLOUT: A patient once asked our physician guest whether she should trust the doctor in front of her — or the podcast host she'd been listening to for months. That's not an anomaly anymore. That's Tuesday.

## The Alzheimer's Drug Lesson

One of the most eye-opening moments in this episode was the deep dive into aducanumab — the Alzheimer's antibody therapy that made global headlines around 2021 as a "breakthrough." The science made it sound extraordinary: a drug that targets and reduces amyloid-beta proteins in the brain, the hallmark of the disease.

The reality? Patients saw minimal improvement in memory or daily function. The risks — brain swelling, bleeding — were substantial. But by the time the nuanced picture emerged, thousands of people had already read the breathless headlines and asked their doctors for access. Managing those expectations became its own medical challenge.

> PULL QUOTE: "A p-value less than 0.05 doesn't mean a treatment will actually help you in real life."
> — Hot Mic, Episode 1

## TikTok Tics, Supplement Culture & Placebo Power

We also got into the genuinely wild phenomenon of "TikTok tics" — where young people began developing tic-like symptoms after watching influencer videos during the COVID pandemic. Neurological exams revealed these weren't true tics at all, but functional neurological responses. One viral video. A global symptom wave. That's the reach of social media in a healthcare context.

And then there's supplements. The billion-dollar industry running on celebrity endorsements and colorful packaging. A study in the New England Journal of Medicine found that for people eating a balanced diet, most vitamins are essentially redundant — food sources are better absorbed anyway. But placebo effects are real and powerful, and sometimes that psychological benefit can mask something that actually needs medical attention.

## Your Cheat Sheet: Red Flags in Research

01 — Tiny sample sizes — a study of 30 people cannot speak for millions.
02 — One dramatic paper with no replication — breakthrough findings need independent confirmation.
03 — No comparison group — "better than nothing" is a very low bar.
04 — Statistical significance ≠ clinical significance — a p-value won't tell you if it'll actually help you.
05 — The word "miracle" in any headline, ever.
06 — Influencers citing single studies without context — check if the broader research community agrees.
07 — Publication bias — negative results rarely get published, which skews the picture.
08 — A degree doesn't equal authority — check if they completed their training.

---

## The Bottom Line

Doctors aren't gatekeeping miracle cures. The FDA fast-tracks genuinely promising treatments. When physicians push back on a supplement or therapy, it's not cynicism — it's context. What often fails to reach the public is what happens after the headline: the side effects, the failed replications, the retracted papers.

The goal of this episode wasn't to make you distrust everything — it was the opposite. If you can walk away asking slightly better questions, approaching that next health claim with a little more curiosity and a little less certainty, that's the win. Bridging the gap between research and real life is hard. But it's possible. And conversations like this one are a start.

Tune in — and next time your algorithm serves you a "doctors don't want you to know" video, you'll know exactly what questions to ask.
