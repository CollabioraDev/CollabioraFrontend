*This episode started on a hiking trail in Santa Monica — two millennials catching their breath and sharing something heavier than the incline: a growing fear that cancer isn't waiting for them to turn 50.*

Colorectal cancer rates in young adults are climbing. That's not a social media scare story — it's a documented medical trend. And in this episode of Hot Mic, our guests do something rare: they talk about it honestly, without softening the edges.

---

## Why Young People?

The usual suspects are increasingly familiar: ultra-processed foods packed with additives, sedentary lifestyles, added sugars lurking in almost everything on American shelves. Food regulations in the US remain significantly looser than in many other countries, and that regulatory gap has real consequences for what ends up in our bodies over decades.

Add in the cultural pressure to normalize digestive discomfort — to call it stress, to call it IBS, to call it "just how my body is" — and you have a recipe for delayed diagnosis. One of our speakers shares exactly that experience: years of unusual symptoms quietly written off, until she decided to stop accepting someone else's explanation for what her body was telling her.

> CALLOUT: One speaker had chalked up years of digestive symptoms to IBS — until she started questioning whether any of it was actually normal *for her body*. That distinction matters more than most people realise.

---

## Symptoms Worth Knowing

Official screening guidelines typically start at age 45–50. But symptoms don't check your date of birth. Here's what this episode flags as worth taking seriously — especially if they're new or persistent for you:

**Changes in Bowel Habits** — Any notable or unexplained shift warrants a conversation with your doctor.

**Blood in Stool** — Darker blood suggests a higher location; bright red suggests lower. Neither is normal.

**Persistent Bloating** — Especially if it's new, worsening, or accompanied by discomfort.

**Night Sweats & Unintentional Weight Loss** — Unexplained and sustained — these are signals that deserve investigation.

**Loss of Appetite** — Unexplained changes in appetite, especially sustained ones, are worth flagging.

## Your Doctor Isn't Always Right — and That's Okay to Say

One of the most important threads in this episode: *patient advocacy*. Too often, unusual symptoms get dismissed as anxiety, hormones, or IBS — disproportionately so for women. The message here is clear: if something doesn't feel right to you, trust that. Push for evaluation. You are not being difficult. You are being responsible.

The healthcare system was not designed to give patients unlimited time. Appointments are short. Doctors are stretched. That means the burden of persistence often falls on the patient — which isn't fair, but is real. Knowing that going in changes how you show up to those appointments.

> PULL QUOTE: "If something doesn't feel normal to you, trust that instinct and get it checked."
> — Hot Mic, Episode 2

## The ALS Conversation

The episode takes a deeper turn when ALS enters the picture. Our neurologist guest reflects on early-career encounters with the disease — part of a broader family of neurodegenerative conditions (alongside Alzheimer's and Parkinson's) where proteins malfunction and accumulate in the nervous system, progressively damaging motor control.

What makes ALS particularly striking is how it can affect the front of the brain — the area responsible for motivation and volition. Patients may appear emotionally flat or detached in response to their own diagnosis. Family members often react far more intensely. It's a condition that reshapes not just the body, but the psychology of everyone around it.

The Ice Bucket Challenge raised significant public awareness and research funding for ALS. For many years, only riluzole was available — newer drugs like edaravone have since extended survival modestly, but there is still no cure.

---

## When "Treatment" Doesn't Mean "Cure"

Perhaps the most quietly devastating part of this episode is the discussion around terminal illness and medical expectations. Many patients — and their families — enter treatment assuming the goal is cure. Often, it isn't. Sometimes palliative chemotherapy or radiotherapy is offered purely to manage symptoms. Sometimes the realistic gain is measured in months, not years.

**Nobody tells patients they can say no.** That patients have a genuine right to decline treatment. That they are not obligated to pursue aggressive intervention if it doesn't align with how they want to live the time they have. The system, as our guests describe it, doesn't communicate this clearly — and that silence does real harm.

> CALLOUT: When treatment fails, patients sometimes blame themselves. This is psychologically devastating and entirely unjust. Realistic expectations — communicated honestly and early — are an act of care, not cruelty.

---

## Your Body, Your Call

The throughline of this episode is autonomy. Not just the medical kind — but the fundamental human right to understand what's happening in your own body, to be given real information, and to make choices that reflect your own values. Not your doctor's values. Not your family's. Yours.

Here's what that looks like in practice:

01 — Don't normalise symptoms that feel abnormal *for you* — your baseline is the reference point, not a textbook average.
02 — Push back on dismissals. Ask for a second opinion. Document your symptoms over time to bring to appointments.
03 — Ask your doctor directly: is this treatment aimed at curing me, or managing symptoms? What does success actually look like?
04 — You are allowed to decline treatment. You are allowed to ask what happens if you don't pursue a particular option.
05 — Awareness matters — colorectal cancer screening guidelines begin at 45, but symptoms don't. Know what to watch for earlier.

This episode doesn't have a tidy resolution — because medicine rarely does. But it leaves you with something arguably more useful than comfort: the vocabulary and the conviction to advocate for yourself, whatever you're facing.
